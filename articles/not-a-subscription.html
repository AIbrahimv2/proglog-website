Why ProgLog is NOT a Subscription

I'm customer number one of ProgLog. I built it because I lift, I love lifting, and I wanted a tool that treated that hobby with the respect it deserves, not one that held it hostage behind a recurring charge.

Before ProgLog was an app on the App Store, it was a tool I built for myself. I'm a lifter. I care about progress, I care about the numbers, and I care about showing up consistently over years, not weeks. I wanted software that matched how I actually train.

What I found on the market was the opposite. Clean ideas buried under paywalls. Simple features locked behind "pro" tiers. Every app, no matter how thin, wanting a slice of my bank account every month in perpetuity. It felt less like software and more like renting the right to log my own workouts.

So I built ProgLog for myself first. And somewhere along the way I realized other people might want it too, but only if I stayed true to why it existed in the first place.

Innovation is not complication

There's a lie the software industry has been telling itself for years: that real innovation requires perpetually complicated, perpetually expensive products. That the way to grow a business is to make development artificially harder, slice features into tiers, and invent problems whose only solution is to pay more.

I don't believe that. Innovation is the opposite. It's looking at something that should be simple, and refusing to make it complicated just because complication makes more money.

A workout tracker should log your workouts. It should show you your progress. It should help you push a little harder next time. Everything beyond that is scope creep dressed up as a feature list.

The phone in your pocket is a supercomputer

The device you carry with you every day is more powerful than the machines that sent people to the moon. It has a faster processor, more memory, and more sensors than the computer most of us grew up with. And yet the industry keeps pretending we need server-side everything, cloud syncing for data you generated three seconds ago, and subscriptions to justify the compute.

ProgLog runs on your phone. Your data lives on your phone. The work happens on your phone. Not because I'm being austere, but because that's what the hardware is actually capable of. Pretending otherwise is willful ignorance.

Everyone else in the industry is quietly ignoring the supercomputer in your pocket so they have an excuse to keep billing you. I refuse to turn a blind eye to what these devices can actually do.

Some things should be subscriptions. This isn't one of them.

I want to be clear: subscriptions aren't evil. There are services that genuinely warrant one: products that run real infrastructure on your behalf, stream content that's licensed month to month, or provide ongoing human work you'd otherwise pay for directly. In those cases, a recurring charge matches a recurring cost. That's fair.

A workout tracker is not that. Once the app is on your phone, there is no recurring cost on my side that scales with your use of it. Charging you every month for software that runs entirely on hardware you already own is asking you to fund my future work, not the product you're actually buying today.

And that's the part that bothers me most. Development cost should never be the reason a product becomes a subscription. When you buy something, you're buying what's in front of you, not pre-paying for work that hasn't happened yet, that you can't see, and that you have no guarantee will ever materialize. Future production is my problem to solve, not a line item on your bill.

A local-first app on a subscription is a trust problem

When a product runs entirely on your device, stores its data on your device, and costs the developer nothing extra when you open it for the thousandth time, a monthly charge starts to look like something else entirely.

The pricing isn't tied to a recurring cost. It's tied to the hope that you'll forget to cancel, that the switching cost is high enough to keep you paying, or that your data being locked inside the app is leverage enough to keep you from leaving. That isn't a product strategy. That's a retention trap wearing a pricing model as a costume.

If you genuinely believe in a local-first product you've built, you should be willing to let it stand on its own. You should want people to use it because they love it, recommend it because it earned it, and come back because it's good, not because a recurring charge is the only thing keeping them tethered to it.

That's the bar I'm holding ProgLog to. Not "no subscriptions ever, for anyone." Just: not for this kind of product, and not from me.

What ProgLog is instead

ProgLog is a one-time purchase. You buy it once, you own it. No monthly fee. No unlocking basic features with a "pro" upsell. No ads. No data harvesting. No dark patterns.

If I add something new and it's big enough to be worth charging for, it'll be a separate purchase, and it'll be optional. The core app stays yours.

I'm not trying to build an empire. I'm trying to build a tool I'd be proud to hand to a friend who asked me how I tracked my lifts. That's the whole bar.

Customer number one

I use ProgLog every time I train. Every set, every rep, every working weight gets logged in the app I've built. If it's missing a feature I need, I add it. If something feels slow or clunky, I fix it, because I'm the one using it the next time I train.

That's the deal. I'm not building this for quarterly reports. I'm building it for myself, and for anyone else who's tired of watching their hobbies get turned into recurring charges on a credit card statement.

ProgLog is not a subscription. And it never will be.

— Abdel-Rahman Ibrahim
ProgLog Founder, Customer #1
